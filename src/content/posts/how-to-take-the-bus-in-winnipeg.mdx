**Riding the bus for the first time is daunting.**

Going into it without much prior experience is terrifying. I struggled to find resources on how to use this, so I hope this guide serves as a quick run-down on understanding this system!

## Fares

Taking the bus (unfortunately) costs money. In our buses, the fare box takes either cash, bus tickets, or Peggo card. **Credit and debit cards aren't accepted here!**

To get bus tickets, check out <Link href="https://info.winnipegtransit.com/en/fares/where-to-buy/">Where to Buy - Winnipeg Transit</Link>, and find the closest place near you.

- **Cash:** There will be a little slot on the bottom right to insert coins. Pay in exact change, as the bus driver does not carry change. Otherwise, you'll overpay.
  - **Paying cash costs more than bus tickets or a Peggo card!** If possible, use bus tickets or a Peggo card if you want to save some money.
- **Bus tickets:** There will be another slot on thet top right, used to accept bus tickets.
- **Peggo card:** The fare box will have a rectangular scanner. Simply tap the scanner to pay, and you'll hear a beep confirming your payment.

Once paid either by bus ticket or cash, you should receive a **paper transfer**, which will print on the left side of the fare box. This is important, but we'll get to that soon though!

If you're strapped on money, there's programs for reduced fares. If you're a student, you can take advantage of the Youth Fare pricing. If you're eligible and paying with cash, you should carry your photo ID. You may also want to check out <Link href="https://info.winnipegtransit.com/en/fares/winnpass">WINNpass</Link>, especially if you're on <dfn id="eia">Employment and Income Assistance</dfn> (<abbr>EIA</abbr>).

For more info and current pricing, check out <Link href="https://info.winnipegtransit.com/en/fares/transit-fares/">Transit Fares</Link>.

## Routing a Trip

Use <Link href="https://www.google.com/maps/">Google Maps</Link> for the best experience of bus routing, that doesn't rely on Google. I was not able to find a better app for bus routing. Sorry, privacy advocates.

Simply put your destination, press "Directions", enter your starting point, then select the transit icon on top.

### Getting On and Off the Bus

Once you pay your fare (more on that later!), you can now sit down on the bus. Generally, you want to go to the back of the bus. However, depending on how crowded it is, this may not be necessary. If you're on a wheelchair, the bus driver will lower a platform for you to be able to get on.

You can check Google Maps to see where you are in your trip.

Once you're about to reach your stop, wait to make sure you've passed the stop before yours. Once passed, you can now pull down the yellow cord on the sides of the bus.

Alternatively, you can press a red stop button found on certain poles. For wheelchair users, a stop button can be found on the sides of folded-up seats, at the front of the bus. If the seats are folded down, folding them up should reveal the button.

### The bus isn't here, what happened?

Sometimes, the bus was early and passed by you. There are also times where buses may get cancelled. This can be incredibly annoying.

Winnipeg Transit and Google Maps can tell you when the next bus will come. You can also call the <Link href="tel:2042877433">TeleBUS number</Link> that's put on every bus stop sign, to figure out when your bus will arrive too.

### What happens if I get lost?

If you have mobile data, simply reroute from your location to where you want to go. If you don't, see if there's any public Wi-Fi nearby.

You may want to come up with an excuse to parents of how you might've gotten lost, if you're transporting yourself to a place that you'd rather keep private.

You may also wish to ask strangers for help. Admittedly, it's a bit scary, but it's better than being stranded. If you just realized you made a wrong turn, you can also talk to the bus driver.

A strategy for if you get lost is to stay on said bus, until you wrap around to your previous location. Otherwise, getting off may be an option if you know you can walk to the next stop or your destination.

## Transfers

### Paper Transfers

Once you pay your fare, the bus driver should give you a paper transfer.

<figure>
	<Image
		alt="Paper transfer"
		class="mx-auto flex max-w-sm items-center justify-center rounded-md object-cover shadow"
		src={PaperTransfer}
	/>
	<figcaption class="mx-auto max-w-lg text-center italic">
		A paper transfer.
	</figcaption>
</figure>

**Make sure to take your paper transfer!**

The reason you want to have this transfer, is then you'll be able to take another bus without having to spend another ticket for a period of time.

To reach your destination, you may need to take multiple buses to get there. You can avoid spending another ticket, by putting the transfer's QR code face-first on the scanner.

You'll notice that your transfer has a date and time issued, and how much time until it expires. Once it expires, you'll need to use another ticket, as the machine won't accept it any longer.

If it expired recently though (say, 15 minutes), and you have one more bus, you can ask the bus driver to let you on anyway.

If for some reason the driver hasn't given you a transfer, simply ask.

### Peggo Cards

If you're using a Peggo card, you won't receive a paper transfer. **Instead, your card will now act as a transfer.**

It won't charge you again when changing buses, for a period of time. You can verify this by changing buses and scanning your Peggo card as normal. The blue display will tell you when your transfer will expire.

## Types of Buses

### Regular buses

Regular buses are the main type of bus you see. In transit apps, these have white backgrounds with their number.

Although technically there's such things such as feeder routes, we'll ignore these for simplicity. Transit apps label these as the same as regulars, and have no functional difference.

### BLUE buses

BLUE buses are part of a rapid transit line, which come by fairly frequently. In transit apps these have blue backgrounds with the text "BLUE".

### Split Lines

You'll see some buses, but with a vertical line split down multiple of them. This means that you could take any one of these buses, to complete your route. These can be any color or type, but usually are branch buses.

## Conclusion

Hopefully this guide serves as a useful stepping stone into using our public transit!

Sadly, driving a car is generally a significantly superior option here, rather than relying solely on public transit. This especially sucks if you're low income, where car ownership is cost-prohibitive. If and/or when you can, advocate for better public infrastructure!

If you wish to save money over time, get to your destination faster and more predictably, consider riding a bike.
