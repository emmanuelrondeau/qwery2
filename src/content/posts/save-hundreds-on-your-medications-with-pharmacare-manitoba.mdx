**If you're not on insurance, once you start increasing your dosage, medications will get _expensive_.**

On 6 mg of oral estradiol (roughly the highest oral dosage you'll get), this will cost roughly $150 Canadian dollars as of writing. This will get you 270 tablets, which lasts for 90 days. This also ignores the cost of an anti-androgen/T-blocker, like Cyproterone or Spironolactone. For me, Cyproterone for 90 days costs roughly $20.

Over a year, **this would cost you roughly $690!** That's a lot of money.

Luckily, you can save yourself a significant amount of money (and get money back!) with the Manitoba Pharmacare Program.

## What's Pharmacare?

Pharmacare is a drug benefit program, where you pay some of the costs of medications, and the Manitoba government will cover the rest.

If you're low income, you'll likely get a $100 deductible. This means that if your medication costs total $500 a year, you will pay $100, and then the Manitoba government will pay the rest. **This means you would save $400!**

## Who qualifies?

According to the <Link href="https://www.gov.mb.ca/health/pharmacare/index.html">Manitoba Government website</Link>, you qualify for the Manitoba Pharmacare program if you meet all of the following criteria:

- You are eligible for Manitoba Health coverage
- Your prescriptions are not covered by other provincial or federal programs

## How to Apply

You'll need to print out the <Link href="https://www.gov.mb.ca/health/pharmacare/docs/application.pdf">Pharmacare Application and Consent Authorization Form</Link>. You can print this out at a public library for some cost. Alternatively, you have a meeting with a peer supporter at <Link href="https://klinic.mb.ca/">Klinic</Link>, and have them help you fill and mail this form.

In the form, unless you know what you're doing, check Option B. By choosing Option B, you can simply set it and forget it.

Once you've applied, you'll receive a letter confirming your enrolled, along with your deductible. If you need an excuse of receiving a letter to parents, you can say that you heard about it from friends, and that it saves a lot of money. If you're a young adult but not currently on perscription meds that your parents are aware of, you can say that you're doing things adults do. If you're a minor, this excuse might not pass as well.

## When do you get your benefits?

Once you submit your application, it will apply on the next <time dateTime="04-01">April 1st</time> from time of submission. Ideally, you'll submit your Pharmacare application before then, so you can take advantage of Pharmacare sooner.
