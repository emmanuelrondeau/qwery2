**Looking to start medical transition in Winnipeg?** This guide will give you info on how to get you on <dfn id="hrt">hormone replacement therapy</dfn> (<abbr>HRT</abbr>) as fast as possible.

This will cover both going through your primary care practitioner and Klinic.

## Filling out Klinic Intake Forms

Klinic Community Health is an organization that provides many health services, including helping you medically transition.

They can help you not only with <abbr>HRT</abbr>, but if you choose, referrals for gender-affirming surgeries, seeing a speech language pathologist to change your voice, and getting laser hair removal from the neck up, all for free!

To register, you have two options:

1. Go [fill out the PDF](https://klinic.mb.ca/health-care/transhealthklinic/community-members/) on this page, print it, then either:

   - Hand it in via physical mail
   - Drop it off at the front desk

2. Go to Klinic physically, then ask the front desk for the trans health intake form
   to fill out. They will give you a clipboard, and you can pick up a sanitized pen to the left of the front desk.

## Finding a Family Doctor (Optional)

**You don't need a family doctor to start hormones! Klinic usually is enough.**

Having one though can speed things up *significantly*, from four months compared to <time dateTime="PT1Y6M">1.5 years</time>. Klinic will also want to transfer you to your family doctor after a year.

See if you have a family doctor already. If it's safe, you can ask your parents if you do have one.

If its unsafe to ask, or your current doctor themselves may be unsafe, or for any other reason, you can go to the [Family Doctor Finder](https://forms.gov.mb.ca/family-doctor-finder/) to find a new family doctor.

## Approaching your Family Doctor about Starting Hormones (Optional)

**This only applies if you've found a family doctor.** If Klinic gets to you first, you can safely ignore this section.

### Filling Out the Informed Consent Form

There are two separate forms for getting estrogen and testosterone. If you're a transfemme, you likely want estrogen. For transmascs, you likely want testosterone.

- [Consent Form for Estrogen and/or Testosterone Blocking Therapy](https://klinic.mb.ca/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/2022-Consent-Estrogen-and-Androgen-blockers.docx)
- [Consent Form for Testosterone Therapy](https://klinic.mb.ca/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/2022-Consent-Testosterone.docx)

Once you've read over it, print it and fill it out!

<Callout type="Info" typeText="Tip for transfemmes">
	You can get [progestin prescribed with a separate form](https://klinic.mb.ca/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/2022-Consent-for-Progestin-Therapy.docx). It's best practice to wait for roughly a year or so, before adding progestin to your regimen.
</Callout>

If you know your doctor's going to be good about prescribing you <abbr>HRT</abbr>, you may not need to give them the informed consent sheet.

### Handing the Informed Consent Form

If you have found a family doctor, you can then schedule an appointment to them!

Some medical clinics may use Medeo, where you can schedule an appointment online. Otherwise, phone calls will likely be an option.

Hand in that sheet at your doctor's appointment, and say "Klinic wants a doctor to start treatment before they take care of me. The guidelines are on their website and I want to start now if possible."

<Callout type="Info">
	If Klinic gets to you first, they will give you an informed consent form to read over and sign.
</Callout>

### What do you do if your doctor refuses?

If the informed consent sheet is signed, your doctor has no reason not to continue with treatment. If they refuse due to inexperience, mention the [<dfn>World Professional Association for Transgender Health</dfn> (<abbr>WPATH</abbr>) Guidelines](https://www.wpath.org/publications/soc) and/or [Sherbourne Guidelines](https://www.rainbowhealthontario.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/09/Guidelines-FINAL-4TH-EDITION-With-2023-Updates.pdf), as these are thorough, definitive guides that do not require special knowledge or prior experience to follow.

These documents, along with extra info, can be found in the "Primary Care & Hormone Therapy" section of the [Health Care Providers](https://klinic.mb.ca/health-care/transhealthklinic/health-care-providers/) page of Klinic.

If they deny you treatment (ex. outright, or redirecting you to Klinic anyway) you can explain to them that you legally signed that you are informed, have given your consent, and would like to start treatment.

If they refuse again, you should insist they continue with treatment, or have them find a new doctor that will. This is an ethics violation, and you should report that to [<dfn>The College of Physicians & Surgeons of Manitoba</dfn> (<abbr>CPSM</abbr>)](https://cpsm.mb.ca/complaints/complaint-process/submitting-a-complaint). To deny care can cause undue harm to a patient, along with discrimination as you are trans and seeking care for that. If you need a new doctor, go back to the family doctor finder to find a new doctor.

If you need help navigating the complaint process, you can email the [Ombudsman](https://www.gov.mb.ca/fippa/make_complaint.html), and they'll be able to help you.

You can also use [RateMDs](https://www.ratemds.com/) or [Review My Doctor](https://reviewmydoctor.ca/) to warn others who may be seeking a doctor.

## Blood Tests

In the process of being on <abbr>HRT</abbr>, you will be required to do a blood test roughly every four months. A nurse will take a blood sample usually from a vein in your arm, and will roughly take five minutes.

If you're squeamish with needles, mention this to the nurse drawing your blood. One strategy is to look away, and attempt to distract yourself. A nurse may ask you questions like "what do you plan to do after?", which can prove very effective. You may also find that looking at the needle while being inserted is more comfortable. If you know that you faint when your blood is drawn, also let them know.

There may also be food available to help your blood replenish, such as juice boxes and crackers. Klinic has these usually on reserve, so feel free to ask for it!

## Pharmacare (Optional)

If you're low income, and _especially_ if you don't have insurance, [apply for Pharmacare!](/posts/save-hundreds-on-your-medications-with-pharmacare-manitoba) **Pharmacare may save you _hundreds_ of dollars!** It bears repeating; **hundreds.**

Navigate to the article above, for a guide on whether you are eligible for Pharmacare (which is very likely), and how to apply.

<Callout type="Info">
	If you qualify for the <dfn>Non-Insured Health Benefits</dfn> (<abbr>NIHB</abbr>) program, you may get gender-affirming medications fully covered. This generally only applies to First Nations people and Inuks. See [Who is eligible for the Non-Insured Health Benefits program](https://sac-isc.gc.ca/eng/1574187596083/1576511384063) for more info.
</Callout>

## Purchasing Prescription Medications

**In order to obtain prescription medication, you'll need to go to a pharmacy.**

Costco Pharmacy is known for having the cheapest prices. You do not need a Costco membership to use the pharmacy; when an employee in the front asks for membership, say you're going to the pharmacy, and they'll let you go.

Otherwise, go with a pharmacy that's most accessible. The price difference is not very big. Prices will likely not matter much if you're under Pharmacare and/or have insurance.

Expect your first prescription to generally be under $90, unless you're on 4 mg oral tabs of estradiol. Otherwise, you will definitely approach the $100+ mark, assuming no insurance nor Pharmacare.

<Callout type="Caution">
	If you're insured by your parents, and you're not out to them, be warned; by
	using your parents' insurance, **your insurance will notify them that it's
	been used for your medications!**
</Callout>

These will come in a paper bag, with a stapled receipt. If your living situation is unsafe, make sure to **have a stealthy place to store it!**

## Taking <abbr>HRT</abbr> for the First Time

If your pharmacist hasn't discussed how to take your medications for the first time, read the prescription bottle. It will contain a short snippet, likely saying to take it daily if you're on oral estradiol.

<Callout type="Info">
	For transfemmes, your routine may involve taking estradiol at night, and
	spironolactone in the day to avoid needing to urinate at night.
</Callout>

Although horribly dense and with lots of irrelevant info, your first-time prescription will contain an informational paper about your medications. Along with really scary potential (but very unlikely) symptoms to watch out, it'll have info on what to do, if you forget to take your medications at specific times.

To help you remember, you can set up recurring tasks in your calendar to make sure you take your medications at the right time.

**Once you've taken the medication for the first time, congrats! Mark the date, as it will become an important one on your transition journey.**

## What next?

Even if you're on <abbr>HRT</abbr>, you may still wish to talk with Klinic.

This is because if you want to be referred for surgeries, such as facial feminization surgery, vaginoplasty, or breast augmentation, you'll need to do it through them. Otherwise, you'll need a private psychologist, which can cost upwards of $2,000+. You can also get free laser hair removal, if you can have them refer you.
